About Me

Zachary K. Runyon

Student · Strategist · Builder · Bilingual

Provo, Utah  ·  BYU Marriott

Who I Am

I'm Zach Runyon — a pre-business student at Brigham Young University's Marriott School of Business with a minor in French, on track to graduate in April 2028. I've spent the better part of my college years doing things the long way: learning a language until I dreamed in it, knocking on doors until the numbers turned in my favor, and building business systems from scratch when no template existed. The result is an unusual mix of analytical discipline and field-tested grit that I try to bring to every project I touch.

My path through BYU hasn't been straight-line. Before my first semester, I spent two years as a full-time volunteer representative for the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints in Lyon, France — living out of a backpack, leading teams across seven cities in France and Switzerland, and developing a cross-cultural fluency that no classroom could have produced. I returned with a C2-level TrueNorth French score of 9.7/10, a richer understanding of how people make decisions, and a genuine love for the country and its people. That experience colored everything that came after: the way I listen in a consulting engagement, the way I structure a pitch, the way I think about whether a solution will actually hold when it meets real human behavior.

Back at BYU, I've kept my academic record tight — a 3.98 GPA, a merit-based full-tuition scholarship, and a spot on the Marriott School Dean's List — while stacking real-world work alongside coursework. Last summer I was in Parker, Colorado for Embrase Marketing, exceeding sales targets by 50% across nine territories and driving over $108,000 in new recurring annual revenue. That experience taught me that strategy without execution is a document, and execution without strategy is just noise. Earlier this year I joined The Momentum Cycle as a Strategy Consulting intern, where I led market-entry analysis that drove a 300% increase in client retention and personally built a click-only lead funnel that generated a 50% jump in new leads. This summer I'll be applying that same discipline at Northwestern Mutual as a College Financial Representative.

Outside of work and coursework, I stay involved on campus through the Management Consulting Association, the Corporate Strategy Association, the Finance Society, and the French Association. I care about the connections between business, language, and people — and I'm always looking for the next problem worth solving.

What I'm Studying

My Pre-Business curriculum at the Marriott School covers the quantitative and conceptual foundations of business — finance, accounting, operations, organizational behavior — while deliberately leaving room to specialize. I've built that specialization around two threads: strategy and French.

The strategy thread pulls through my consulting coursework, my membership in the Management Consulting Association, and my hands-on work with early-stage companies. The French minor isn't a curiosity — it's a competitive differentiator. In a global business environment, the ability to read a room in a second language, negotiate without a translator, and understand cultural context at a native level opens doors that bilingual PDFs cannot. The combination of analytical business training and genuine linguistic fluency is exactly what I'm building toward.

On the technical side, I work regularly with Excel, PowerPoint, Tableau, SQL, and JavaScript — not as check-the-box skills, but as tools I've reached for in real work contexts. I believe that the best analysts are the ones who can do the thinking and build the deliverable.

Building Systems That Scale

One of the things I find most satisfying about business is designing processes that keep working after you've stopped touching them. At The Momentum Cycle, I didn't just analyze a market opportunity — I built the onboarding flow, the classroom architecture, the gamification layer, and the referral mechanism inside Skool, so that every new member enters a consistent, repeatable experience. At Embrase, I didn't just hit a sales target — I tested and refined my door-to-door approach until the conversion rate went from 6% to 12.5%, then documented what worked.

That instinct for building repeatable systems is something I bring into every engagement. Good strategy without an operational backbone is just a slide deck. I try to close that gap.

Languages & Cross-Cultural Work

Two years of full-time immersion in France and Switzerland will do things to your relationship with language that a classroom simply can't replicate. I was responsible for communicating across seven cities — adapting register, vocabulary, and approach for each demographic I encountered — and for leading teams of 10 to 30 people who came from different countries, backgrounds, and communication styles. My formal TrueNorth score came out at 9.7/10 (C2), but the more valuable credential is the lived experience of navigating ambiguity in a second language at scale.

I use that fluency practically. I've participated in French-language business case discussions, connected with francophone professionals, and engaged with the French Association at BYU to keep the language sharp. For any business context with a French-speaking client base or partner network, I can operate without a handler.

How I Spend My Time Off the Clock

The mountains are my reset. When I'm not in a spreadsheet or a strategy session, I'm most likely chasing powder at Brighton or Snowbird in the Wasatch, or making my way to one of the Colorado resorts I grew up near. Snowboarding taught me risk calibration in a very literal sense — read the terrain, commit to the line, stick the landing — and I've never found a better metaphor for how I try to approach decisions.

Golf is the other obsession. There's something about the combination of precision, patience, and the long feedback loop that appeals to the same part of my brain that likes building strategy frameworks. Basketball and baseball have always been in the mix too — team sports, competitive games, the kind of things that remind you how much execution depends on everyone around you. And when I need to clear my head, I run. Nothing fancy, just miles.

The common thread across all of it: I like things with a score, a feedback loop, and room to improve. That's probably why I ended up in business.
